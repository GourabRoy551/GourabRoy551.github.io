Two Days in Bordeaux

I went to the first-ever IPCVAI Days in Bordeaux not fully knowing what to expect. I came back with a clearer picture of where this program leads, and a much stronger feeling of actually belonging to something.

IPCVAI Bordeaux Community Computer Vision Erasmus Mundus

Getting to Bordeaux was already a small adventure. I had only been in Europe for less than a year at that point, and traveling to a French city I had never visited, for an event I could not fully picture in my head, felt like one of those things you just say yes to before your brain talks you out of it.

The IPCVAI Days 2026 was the first edition of what the consortium hopes will become a regular gathering — a place for current students, alumni, professors, and industry people who have passed through this program to actually sit in the same room together. Two days at LaBRI, Université de Bordeaux, June 3rd and 4th. I registered my name badge, held it in my hand, and felt something shift a little. Gourab Roy. Bangladesh. 2025–2027. That is who I am in this program.

Day One — Finding Your Footing

The first morning started with coffee and the quiet buzz of people who mostly did not know each other yet. There were students from all the current cohorts, professors who had built the program, and alumni who had flown in from across Europe. It was one of those situations where everyone is slightly unsure where to stand, so everyone just migrates toward the coffee table.

The opening was given by Fabien Baldacci from LaBRI's Image and Sound Department. He talked about the first edition of these days — what the event was meant to be, why the community needed something like this, and how both the program and its people had grown since the beginning. Watching the room fill up, listening to him describe the history of IPCVAI, it was easy to forget that most of us had only been students here for a short time. The screen behind him just said IPCV Days 2026: 1st edition — simple and honest about what it was.

After the opening talk, the floor opened up for poster presentations — and this is where the day became genuinely interesting. There were students from multiple cohorts presenting work across all sorts of directions: detection, segmentation, generative models, video understanding, medical imaging. Walking from poster to poster, having five-minute conversations that sometimes stretched into twenty, asking questions you might feel awkward asking in a lecture — it is a completely different register of learning. You realize that the person standing next to a confusing diagram is usually as uncertain about parts of it as you are. That is weirdly reassuring.

The afternoon brought a talk from Kristof Káracs on computer vision research at PPKE, which gave a useful window into how a partner lab approaches these problems. Then more posters. Then a coffee break that turned into a conversation that ran well past the scheduled end time. The day closed with an aperitif — drinks on the terrace, the city of Bordeaux somewhere in the background, people finally getting comfortable enough to stop being professional at each other.

Between Days — A Rainy Bordeaux Evening

That evening I walked around Bordeaux a little. It started raining — the kind of light, steady French rain that does not care whether you have an umbrella or not. The streets near the waterfront were quiet and wet and genuinely beautiful in a way that felt unplanned. I passed through the old medieval gate, the Porte Cailhau, and stood there for a minute just looking at it. There is something strange and good about being in a city with a thousand years of history and knowing you are there because of a master's program in computer vision.

I shot a short clip at one of the spots I wandered through — not sure I can name exactly where, but it had that quality old French cities sometimes have where even a thirty-second video makes the place look like it belongs in a film.

Day Two — The Alumni Who Changed Everything

If Day 1 was about meeting the current community, Day 2 was about seeing where it goes.

The morning opened with a talk on the VPU Lab by Jesús Cano and José María Martínez — the Video Processing and Understanding Lab in Madrid, which is one of the partner institutions I know well. Hearing them present in this setting, to an audience of students from across the whole program, was different from a regular lecture. It felt more like a report back: here is what we have been building, here is why it matters.

Then the alumni presentations started, and this is the part of the two days I keep thinking about.

Sebastián Cajas, now at MIT Critical Data and Workday, presented on shortcut learning and human blind spots in model behavior — a genuinely unsettling talk about how models learn to be confident in the wrong places. Tamas Bukits from Vicomtech talked about bringing real-world entities into immersive environments. Nour Mohamed Abdelhamed, now a machine learning engineer at TRATON AB, talked about what the gap between academia and industry actually looks like when you are standing in the middle of it — not from a textbook perspective, but from experience.

"From Computer Vision Student to Machine Learning Engineer: Bridging Academia and Industry." That title hit differently when you are in the middle of the student part and can suddenly see people who made it to the other side — and they look like people, not a different species.

In the afternoon, Syed Talal Wasim from the University of Bonn talked about his PhD in video AI — understanding temporal patterns, anticipating what happens next in a sequence. Bolutife Atoki from LIRIS presented work on AI-based detectors for physical security. And Julia Alekseenko from IHU Strasbourg asked what might be the best question in the field right now: can AI actually understand surgery?

These are people who came through the same program. Who sat in similar poster sessions, had the same anxious early months in a new country, and figured it out. Watching them present, answering questions with confidence, talking about their research problems like people who have learned to live with the uncertainty rather than fight it — it was motivating in a way that a career brochure never manages to be.

The Group Photo

At some point someone asked everyone to gather for a group photo. I am not usually someone who particularly enjoys these — there is always the choreography of getting that many people to stand in the right direction while someone adjusts a camera — but this one felt worth it. When I looked at the room, I could see people from probably a dozen different countries, from at least three different cohorts, with professors who have spent careers building the field and students who are still building their first models. The photo does not capture the noise or the warmth or the fact that five different conversations were still happening while we were supposed to be smiling. But it captures that we were all there.

What I Am Taking Back

I came to Bordeaux mostly curious and a little nervous. I left with something harder to name. It is not just the talks, although those were genuinely good. It is not just the networking, which is a word that makes every real conversation sound like a transaction.

It is more that I now have a much more concrete picture of what this program looks like end-to-end. I have seen the people who came before me — where they went, what they are working on, how they talk about problems, what they seem to have kept from the program and what they have learned to let go of. That is genuinely rare. Most of the time in a master's program you are looking at a two-year window and have no real visibility beyond it.

I also just feel more settled in the community. Before this, the IPCVAI program was, for me, largely defined by the specific university I was attending and the specific courses I was taking. After Bordeaux, it feels bigger than that. It is a network of people who studied similar things in similar circumstances across different cities and cohorts, and who are now spread out doing genuinely interesting work. I am early in that timeline. But at least now I can see the shape of it.

Bordeaux was good. The rain was fine. The medieval gate was beautiful. The coffee was French and therefore strong. And the conversations — the real ones, not the scheduled ones — were the part I did not expect to value as much as I did.
